Bio

I am currently a PhD candidate in Mechanical Engineering at Boston University department of Mechanical Engineering under supervision of Dr. Harold Park. I graduated from University at Buffalo-SUNY with Master's of Science in Mechanical Engineering under supervision of Dr. David Salac.

My current research project is to develop theoretical and computational frameworks to study surface instabilities that occur on dielectric elastomers under both mechanical and electrical loading while subjected to surface tension.

Research Summary

Soft materials response to electrical loadings has been always of interest to scientists and engineers. Example includes from the electroporation of biological membranes to mechanical instability of dielctric elastomers due to an applied electric field.

I am interested in using computational techniques to study the behavior of such systems. I am studying elastocapillary effects on the behavior of dielectric elastomers subjected to electric field (Electro-Elasto-Capillary). I used to develope phase-field models in order to study the electroporation of biological membranes.

Research Projects

  • Electro-Elasto-Capillary Model of Dielectric Elastomer

    I am studying the effect of surface tension over dielectric elastomers subjected to an applied electric field using newly developed finite deformation, dynamic finite element method.

  • Surface tension role in wrinkling instability

    I am developing theoretical model augmented with computational analysis to examine the surface instabilities of dielectric elastomers under compression accounting for surface tension effects.

  • Electroporation of Biological Membrane

    I've been using diffuse interface approach (i.e. phase-field) in order to model the electroporation of a lipid membrane.

Computational Modeling of Electro-Elasto-Capillary Phenomena in Dielectric Elastomers

Saman Seifi, Harold S. Park
Journal Paper International Journal of Solids and Structures, Volume 87, Jan 2016, Pages 236–244

Abstract

We present a new finite deformation, dynamic finite element model that incorporates surface tension to capture elastocapillary effects on the electromechanical deformation of dielectric elastomers. We demonstrate the significant effect that surface tension can have on the deformation of dielectric elastomers through three numerical examples: (1) surface tension effects on the deformation of single finite elements with homogeneous and inhomogeneous boundary conditions; (2) surface tension effects on instabilities in constrained dielectric elastomer films, and (3) surface tension effects on bursting drops in solid dielectrics. Generally, we find that surface tension creates a barrier to instability nucleation. Specifically, we find in agreement with recent experimental studies of constrained dielectric elastomer films a transition in the surface instability mechanism depending on the elastocapillary length. The present results indicate that the proposed methodology may be beneficial in studying the electromechanical deformation and instabilities for dielectric elastomers in the presence of surface tension.
